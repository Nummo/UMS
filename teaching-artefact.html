Idea: Fadeouts to Blackouts - how open is the world to you?

Sitting in higher education institutions in the UK in dialogues about mobility and mobilities can produce an assumption that by dint of sharing this space, similar approaches and access to national and international movement is shared. This teaching artefact opens a dialogue on the idea of an open world and the openness of the world. The use of maps can present the world, a country, a localised place as open and expansive, yet there are a lot physical and metaphysical boundaries and barriers. These boundaries and barriers are what we will be using to create our own 'Open World Map'.

Activities

  • Create your Fadeout to Blackout criteria
  • Sketch out your Open World Map: using a Mercator projection map/ sketch of the world using a three- tone key.

Creating a Fadeout to Blackout criteria

The 'No'

Your 'No' criteria is be based on what you feel is important. As critical theory is central to this discussion, you may wish to consider intersectional factors. If you wish to adopt an information system based approach, you may wish to consider how visa-free the world is to you and others who you know (family, friend, community).

A layered openness

Positioning is a key element of this activity. To thing of ourselves as multi-selves to (in effect) create a character profile. However, not all positions can be comfortably expressed and shared, so with this there are a couple of caveats. Positioning yourself in the 'Open World Map' can come in different ways. One obvious way is to place yourself as centre. Another is to place someone you know as the centre (no government names or real names please). Another is to take a person from the past, a historic figure. Or a fictional character that can be situated within a Mercator projection map. In summary, there are degrees of openness and if you feel that this space is not appropriate for a certain part of yourself - keep and stay firm with that boundary.
